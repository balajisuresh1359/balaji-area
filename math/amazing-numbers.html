Amazing Numbers

Updated on: April 26, 2025

Numbers are always amazing. Some numbers, equations, series, and patterns behave differently under certain operations. Even though all numbers follow general mathematical rules, some patterns surprise us. Letâ€™s explore a few of these cases.

1. Product and Sum Are the Same

Have you ever seen numbers where the sum and product are equal? We know that:

\[ 2 + 2 = 2 \times 2 \]

But are there more such pairs? Yes! There are infinitely many such pairs that satisfy:

\[ a + b = a \times b \]

Some examples are:

\[ (5, 1.25), \quad (6, 1.2), \quad (7, 1.1666), \dots \]

The general formula for such pairs is:

\[ \left(n, \frac{n}{n-1}\right) \]

which satisfies:

\[ n \times \frac{n}{n-1} = n + \frac{n}{n-1} \]

Similarly

\[ n \times \frac{n}{n+1} = n - \frac{n}{n+1} \]

2. Square Root Pattern

There exists a number for which:

\[ \sqrt{x} + \sqrt{x+1} = \sqrt{x+2} \]

Solving this equation, we get:

\[ x \approx 0.154700538 \]

3. Sum of a Number and Its Square Equals Its Cube

In school, we often mistakenly assume:

\[ x^a + x^b = x^{a+b} \]

which is incorrect. However, there exists a special number for which:

\[ x + x^2 = x^3 \]

That number is the famous Golden Ratio:

\[ x \approx 1.618033989 \]

4. The Range of Nth Power Roots

For any natural number \( n \), the nth root of \( n \) always lies in the range:

\[ 1 \leq n^{\frac{1}{n}} \leq 1.442 \]

Similarly, its reciprocal follows:

\[ 0.5 \leq \frac{1}{n^{\left(\frac{1}{n}-1\right)}} < 1 \]
